MEDIAN HOUSEHOLD INCOME GAP

About

The Median Household Income Gap measures the gap or the ratio between the median income of households headed by a Black individual versus the median income of households headed by a White individual. Individuals from both Hispanic and non-Hispanic ethnicities are included. The Median Household Income Gap highlights systemic inequitable in economic opportunities and outcomes, rooted in historical and structural discrimination. This measure is crucial for understanding disparities that affect community health, resource access, and quality of life. According to the U.S. Census Bureau’s 2022 data, the median household income for Black Households was $52,860, while for White households was $81,060. This indicates that Black households earned approximately 65% of what White households earned, highlighting a significant income disparity between these groups [1].

Why is the Median Household Income Gap important to structural and institutional racism and discrimination?

Overview

The Median Household Income Gap is crucial to understanding structural and institutional racism and discrimination because it reflects disparities in income, employment, homeownership, education, access to credit, and retirement savings between Black and White households [2]. This gap is not solely due to labor market disadvantages but is significantly influenced by national trends of rising income inequality, which, if not for its increase, would have seen a 30% reduction in the median black-white family income gap [3]. Institutional racism, particularly in the 20th century, has played a pivotal role in creating disparities in homeownership and house values, contributing to current racial inequalities [4]. Moreover, structural racism affects family structure and exacerbates poverty and inequality [5], underlining the importance of addressing these systemic issues to close the income gap and combat racial discrimination.

Expected Relation to Structural Racism and Discrimination

A higher gap between the median income of Black households versus White households potentially indicates a higher inequality of income and lower wealth accumulation for the Black population to the White population. Higher income inequality (and low accumulation of wealth) among the Black population increases the occurrences and risk of experiencing social injustices and discrimination and further amplifies the manifestation of structural and instructional racism and discrimination.

How is the Median Household Income Gap calculated?

Data

We used two variables, 1) Median income in US dollars of households headed by an individual from the Black race, and 2) Median income in US dollars of households headed by an individual from the White race. Individuals from both Hispanic and non-Hispanic ethnicities are included.

Methodology

We calculated the Median Household Income Gap using a ratio formula.

RBWhMedHHInc = BlMedHHInc WhMedHHInc

Where:

RBlWhMedHHInc: Ratio between the median income of Black households versus median income of White households

BlMedHHInc: Median Household Income for Black population

WhMedHHInc: Median Household Income for White population

Note: A high Median Household Income Gap (RBlWhMedHHInc) corresponds to a high SRD Index Score.

Missing Data

We replaced missing values by using functions that were rooted in Tobler's First Law of Geography, i.e., "everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things" [5]. This law also informs the concept of Spatial Autocorrelation (SA). The indicators were highly spatially autocorrelated and the missing values were imputed using the median value of the eight nearest neighbors. The eight nearest neighbors were identified using the KDTree algorithm in Python's scipy.spatial module. The KDTree is a data structure used to organize points in a k-dimensional space for efficient nearest-neighbor searches. It is a binary tree built by recursively splitting the dataset along different axes, ensuring balanced partitions using the “sliding midpoint” rule. Each node represents an axis-aligned hyperrectangle, and the tree structure allows for efficient querying of the 'n' closest neighbors to a given point [6]. After imputation of missing data, we have 13 counties with no data in 2020, and none for the 2010 and 2000 years.
